## Indonesia Smartphone 2025 Export data: Latest Trend

Indonesia has recently undergone significant policy updates affecting smartphone exports, including the enforcement of the Tingkat Komponen Dalam Negeri (TKDN) regulation mandating 35% local content and the introduction of Permendag 16/2025, which digitalizes trade processes through a risk-based system [[DHL](https://www.dhl.com/discover/en-id/small-business-advice/growing-your-business/how-to-import-mobile-phone-in-indonesia)]. These changes, along with a reported 10% import duty despite WTO zero-rate commitments, have introduced compliance complexities and potential trade barriers [[Tempo](https://en.tempo.co/read/1999751/8-trade-barriers-in-indonesia-according-to-the-us)]. Reflecting this regulatory volatility, monthly export values for Indonesia smartphone Export under HS code 8517 showed notable fluctuations in 2025, ranging from a low of 5.31B USD in April to a peak of 6.87B USD in July, with a total export value of approximately 62.18B USD from January to October, suggesting that policy shifts may be driving market adjustments and shipment timing.

Smartphones, represented by HS code 8517, are wireless communication devices integral to industries such as telecommunications, consumer electronics, and digital services, catering to both individual and enterprise users. Global demand remains strong due to the product's role in enabling connectivity, supporting economic activities, and driving technological adoption worldwide, with smartphones being essential tools in the rapidly expanding digital infrastructure.

Indonesia matters as a key exporter of smartphones due to its strategic position in Southeast Asia, competitive manufacturing base, and focus on local content development through policies like TKDN, which enhance its regional trade presence. However, deep analysis of the HS sub-codes within 8517 is necessary to dissect the export composition, as it includes a mix of high-value finished devices and lower-value components, requiring detailed examination to accurately assess market trends and compliance with evolving regulations.

## Indonesia Smartphone 2025 Export data: HS Code Breakdown

The **Indonesia smartphone Export** market under HS code **8517** is overwhelmingly dominated by high-value finished products, with sub-code **85171300** (Telephone sets; smartphones for cellular or other wireless networks) accounting for 45.72% of total export value at a premium unit price of $273.90 per piece, indicating a focus on exporting premium, fully assembled devices rather than lower-value components or parts.

Beyond finished smartphones, the export structure reveals a significant volume of core communication components and parts, primarily under sub-codes like **85177921** (69.05% of total quantity, $3.84 per piece) and **85177939**, which together suggest large-scale export of mid-to-low value parts likely used in regional assembly operations. This mix—combining high-value end products with high-quantity, lower-cost components—points to an export profile centered on final assembly supplemented by substantial trade in parts, rather than deep local manufacturing of high-end sub-assemblies.

This product mix implies that **Indonesia smartphone Export** under **8517** functions both as an exporter of premium finished goods and a key supplier of assembly-level components, making it strategic for buyers targeting ready-to-market devices or bulk parts for SKD/CKD operations. The high unit price disparity underscores reliance on imported technology for high-end devices, while the volume of parts aligns with Indonesia's role as a regional assembly hub, further emphasized by recent regulatory shifts like the TKDN local content rules and digital compliance frameworks [[DHL](https://www.dhl.com/discover/en-id/small-business-advice/growing-your-business/how-to-import-mobile-phone-in-indonesia)], which may affect sourcing strategies for component-heavy supply chains.

The remaining three clusters reveal distinct supply chain roles: the High-Value-Low-Frequency segment (6.72% value share) suggests infrequent but large orders, potentially for new product launches or major contract wins, as indicated by firms like DA ALL VINA; the Low-Value-High-Frequency cluster (0.83% value share) likely represents aftermarket or maintenance part replenishment for distributors, exemplified by PANASONIC SYSTEM NETWORKS; and the Low-Value-Low-Frequency group (1.39% value share) consists of niche or occasional buyers such as CHERNG WEI VINA, possibly engaging in prototype or small-batch sourcing.

## Indonesia Smartphone 2025 Export data: Action Plan for Market Expansion

### Conclusion: Strategic Overview for Indonesia smartphone Export
Indonesia smartphone Export operates as a dual-strategy market, driven by premium finished device technology (evidenced by HS 85171300's $273.90/unit price) and high-volume OEM contract manufacturing for components (HS 85177921’s 69.05% quantity share at $3.84/unit). Core price drivers are technology specifications and brand premium for finished goods, and volume-based contracts for assembly-level parts. This creates significant supply chain implications: heavy reliance on High-Value-High-Frequency OEM buyers (91.06% of export value) introduces vulnerability to demand shifts or model changes, while geographic concentration—especially the U.S. as a premium endpoint and Vietnam as an assembly hub—requires agile, hub-dependent logistics. Indonesia functions as both a finished goods exporter and a regional SKD/CKD parts supplier, but its limited local high-end sub-assembly manufacturing underscores dependency on imported technology.

### Action Plan: Data-Driven Steps for smartphone Market Execution

- **Prioritize HVHF buyer relationship management** by analyzing transaction histories of top OEMs to anticipate scheduling changes and align production with their contract cycles, securing recurring high-value orders.

- **Diversify into aftermarket and MRO channels** by targeting Low-Value-High-Frequency buyers with optimized digital sales platforms, leveraging their steady demand for components to reduce OEM dependency.

- **Strengthen logistics partnerships with transshipment hubs** like the Netherlands and Vietnam to ensure timely delivery to high-value destinations (e.g., U.S.) and assembly hubs, mitigating geographic concentration risks.

- **Align product certification with Indonesia’s TKDN and digital compliance rules** to maintain access to major OEM supply chains and capitalize on regulatory incentives for local content.

### Indonesia smartphone Export Forward Outlook
Traditional sourcing and selling methods—relying on aggregated trade data or finished goods metrics—fail to capture the critical dynamics of Indonesia smartphone Export, as they overlook SKD/CKD parts flows and individual OEM scheduling that drive market volatility and profitability. The forward outlook points to increasing regional manufacturing consolidation, with Indonesia reinforcing its role as a key supplier for ASEAN assembly networks, but facing pressure from evolving regulations (e.g., TKDN updates) and global tariff shifts. By executing these data-driven actions—deepening OEM integration, diversifying channels, and optimizing logistics—exporters can reduce single-buyer dependency, enhance supply chain resilience, and capitalize on Indonesia's strategic position in the global smartphone value chain.
